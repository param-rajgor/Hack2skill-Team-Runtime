# Candidate Patterns

## Strong Candidates

- Skills align closely with career history and job responsibilities.
- High assessment scores support claimed technical expertise.
- Demonstrate clear career progression from junior to senior roles.
- Possess long-duration skills with strong endorsement counts.
- Education, skills, and work experience tell a consistent professional story.
- Show evidence of specialization rather than random unrelated skills.

---

## Weak Candidates

- Limited evidence supporting claimed skills.
- Low assessment scores despite claiming advanced technical expertise.
- Short skill durations and very few endorsements.
- Little career progression despite many years of experience.
- Generic profiles with minimal specialization.
- Education and work history provide weak support for claimed competencies.

---

## Suspicious Candidates

- Claim advanced AI/ML skills such as NLP, Fine-Tuning LLMs, LoRA, GANs, or RAG without any related work experience.
- Career history and listed skills belong to completely different domains.
- High self-reported proficiency but weak assessment scores.
- Large number of advanced skills acquired within very short durations.
- Skills, education, and experience appear inconsistent with one another.
- Frequent domain switching with no evidence of expertise in any specific area.

---

## Authentic Candidates

- Career history directly supports listed skills.
- Assessment scores validate claimed expertise.
- Education and certifications align with professional experience.
- Skill durations indicate long-term exposure and practical usage.
- Career progression follows a logical growth trajectory.
- Profile information remains consistent across all sections.

---

## Hidden Talent Candidates

- Strong assessment scores despite unconventional career histories.
- Demonstrate transferable skills across domains.
- Show evidence of self-learning through certifications and projects.
- Possess relevant technical skills not reflected in current job titles.
- May be overlooked by keyword-based recruitment systems.
- Require deeper evaluation beyond traditional resume screening.

---

## Interesting Observations

- Several Backend Engineers and Data Engineers list advanced AI/ML skills despite primarily working in traditional software roles.
- Assessment scores appear to be one of the strongest validation signals for claimed technical skills.
- Career-history-to-skill alignment is a strong indicator of profile authenticity.
- Education alone does not appear to be a reliable indicator of candidate quality.
- Candidates with consistent education, experience, and skills tend to appear more credible than candidates with stronger individual signals but poor overall alignment.
- Profile consistency may be more valuable than any single feature when determining candidate authenticity.
- Candidates often claim a larger number of skills than can be reasonably justified by their work history, suggesting skill inflation may be common.
- Work experience provides stronger evidence of expertise than self-reported proficiency levels.
- Candidates with strong technical assessments but unconventional career histories may represent hidden talent overlooked by traditional hiring systems.
- A combination of career-history alignment, skill validation, and profile consistency is likely more predictive of candidate quality than any individual feature.
